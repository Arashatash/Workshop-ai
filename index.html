Before We Start

The Three Zones — Your Compliance Framework

Before you touch any tool, you need one clear mental model for what's safe. These three zones govern every task. Learn them once, apply them to every tool, every prompt, forever. This is your permission slip.

✅
Green Zone — Use Freely
Public information, published research, general knowledge, personal productivity. No sensitive data involved. External tools are fine here.
Examples: Summarise a published clinical paper. Research competitor public announcements. Scan PBAC recommendations. Explore therapy area trends. Prepare for a congress. Draft a personal meeting agenda.
⚠️
Amber Zone — Concierge + Review
Internal documents, HCP communications, content destined for external audiences. AI drafts — you review — compliance checks before anything is sent. Use Concierge for anything internal.
Examples: Draft an HCP email (review before sending). Synthesise internal market research. First draft of a brand plan section. Pre-call planning briefs. Advisory board synthesis. Territory analysis from internal data.
🚫
Red Zone — No AI. Full Stop.
Patient data, unpublished clinical results, NDA-protected information, financial forecasts under confidentiality. No external tools — ever. Not even Concierge.
Examples: Individual patient records or identifiable health data. Unpublished Phase III results. Pre-submission regulatory data. Anything Legal has specifically restricted. Personal health information of any kind.
The simple rule: Public or published → Green (any tool fine). Internal but not restricted → Amber (Concierge, review your output). Patient data, unpublished results, NDA → Red (no AI at all). When in doubt, treat as Amber and use Concierge.
Where compliance lives in your prompts: The C (Constraints) in every SPACE prompt is your compliance layer. Writing "no promotional claims, no off-label language, no unsubstantiated product claims" inside C pre-filters every output before it goes anywhere. Build this habit once — it protects everything automatically.

Module 01

Prompt Smarter

The quality of your output is a direct function of the quality of your input. Most people prompt AI like they Google — minimal context, vague request, no constraints — then blame the tool when the output is generic. This module gives you three things: a framework for every prompt, 8 power techniques most people never discover, and Prompt Decorators — two-character tags that instantly change how the AI thinks.

✗ What most people type

"Write an email to a cardiologist about our product."

✓ What actually gets results

A prompt that tells Concierge who you are, what the output needs to do, who reads it, what constraints to respect — and shows it a tone reference.

📖 EDUCATIONAL: Part 1 — The SPACE Framework
S

Situation — set the context

Tell Concierge who you are and what you're working on. Without this it assumes a generic professional in an unknown industry.

"I am a Customer Engagement Manager for a cardiovascular brand at Sanofi, managing a territory of 35 priority cardiologists across NSW."
P

Purpose — what this output needs to do

Not what it's about — what job it must perform. This shifts output from descriptive to functional.

"Draft a re-engagement email that creates a specific, low-pressure reason for a 10-minute conversation in the next 2 weeks."
A

Audience — who reads it and what they care about

This single element often produces the biggest quality leap. It shifts vocabulary, tone, depth, and emphasis entirely.

"Senior interventional cardiologist — evidence-focused, time-pressured, immediately sceptical of promotional language, responds to peer-level conversations."
C

Constraints — length, format, tone, what to avoid

Your guardrails. In pharma, this is where your compliance rules live inside the prompt. Write them here once — Concierge applies them to every output.

"Maximum 120 words. No product claims requiring MLR review. Professional but conversational. One clear CTA. Amber zone — flag this needs review before sending."
E

Example — a tone reference that calibrates everything

The most underused and highest-impact element. Any sentence you admire becomes a reference. One sentence is enough.

"Match the directness of: 'Hi Dr. Chen, I noticed we haven't connected in a while — I'd value 10 minutes to hear your perspective on [area]. No agenda, just a conversation. Would [date] work?'"
You don't need all five every time. Quick internal task: P + C. High-stakes external communication: all five. The question before every prompt: what does Concierge not know that it needs to know?

📖 EDUCATIONAL: Part 2 — 8 Power Techniques

SPACE structures every prompt. These 8 techniques transform what Concierge can do for you — most people use AI for months without discovering any of them.

Technique 01
Ask me clarifying questions first
Use before any complex task — brand plan sections, competitive briefs, strategic memos. Eliminates the #1 reason AI outputs are generic: the model assumed instead of asked.
Before you start, ask me every question you need answered to give me the best possible output. Don't begin writing until I've answered them.
Technique 02
Summarise what you understood before you answer
For any multi-part or complex request. Catches misunderstandings before you get a wall of wrong output — saves complete rewrites.
Before you respond, summarise in 2 sentences what you think I'm asking for and what success looks like. Then proceed.
Technique 03
Give me three versions: safe, bold, and one I haven't considered
For HCP emails, brand positioning, key messages, campaign angles. Forces Concierge out of the average and gives you real options to choose from.
Give me three versions of this: Version A: The safe, conventional approach Version B: A bolder version that takes a stronger position Version C: An angle I haven't considered that might work better than both
Technique 04
Now argue the opposite
After any strategic draft. The single best technique for stress-testing brand strategy, messaging, or positioning before it goes to leadership.
Now argue the strongest possible case against this position. What would a sceptical CCO or a competing brand manager say? Be direct — don't soften the challenge.
Technique 05
What am I not asking that I should be?
End any research or analysis session with this. Surfaces the blind spots Concierge can see but you haven't thought to ask about. Often the most valuable output of the whole conversation.
Based on everything we've discussed, what are the 2–3 most important questions I haven't asked but should? What am I not seeing?
Technique 06
Write the ideal prompt, then use it
For tasks you do repeatedly — HCP emails, status updates, competitive briefs. Let Concierge design its own brief. The result is usually significantly better than what you'd have written.
Write the ideal SPACE prompt that would produce the best possible [HCP re-engagement email / competitive brief / brand plan section] for my role. Then use that prompt to produce the output.
Technique 07
Inject a persona to stress-test
Before sending any HCP communication or presenting any strategy. Forces Concierge to evaluate from the perspective of your most critical audience — surfaces weaknesses instantly.
Review this draft as a sceptical senior cardiologist who has heard every sales pitch, trusts only peer-reviewed data, and has 30 seconds of patience for anything that sounds promotional. What would she think? What would she dismiss immediately?
Technique 08
Iteration discipline — never stop at the first draft
After any output. The first draft is the starting point, not the destination. This single phrase — used consistently — is worth more than any other technique on this list.
This is good. Now make it better: be 30% more direct, cut anything a senior commercial leader would already know, and make the final sentence land harder.

📖 EDUCATIONAL: Part 3 — Prompt Decorators

Prompt Decorators are simple instructional tags you drop into a prompt (such as +++Reasoning or +++Critique) to instantly shift how the AI thinks, reasons, and writes. Instead of typing paragraphs of instructions on how you want the AI to analyze something, you apply a decorator. It acts as a programmatic "mental mode" for Concierge.

+++Reasoning
Force Logic First
Demands the AI build and explain its logical train of thought before providing an answer. Fixes shallow reasoning and improves accuracy dramatically.
Usage: Start your standard prompt with +++Reasoning
+++Critique
The Hard Review
Forces a balanced structural critique. Concierge will be forced to highlight strengths, ruthlessly expose weaknesses, and suggest concrete improvements.
Usage: Start your standard prompt with +++Critique
+++Socratic
Clarify & Explore
Instead of answering blindly, the AI becomes your coach. It will pose clarifying questions, analyze assumptions, and explore different perspectives with you.
Usage: Start your standard prompt with +++Socratic
+++Tone(style=...)
Instantly Lock Tone
Rather than describing the vibe, declare the exact tone constraint using the style parameter. Ensures perfectly polished prose.
Usage: +++Tone(style=McKinsey-level executive summary)
✗ Standard SPACE Prompt

"S: I am a Brand Manager...
P: Review this brand plan assumption...
A: For my own review...
C: Be direct...
E: Match McKinsey..."

(Result: Solid output, but the AI often agrees with you too easily without questioning the premise.)

✓ SPACE + Prompt Decorators

+++Critique +++Reasoning
"S: I am a Brand Manager...
P: Review this brand plan assumption..."

(Result: Concierge violently brutally tears down the assumption, exposes the flawed logic step-by-step, and rebuilds a stronger strategy.)

📖 EDUCATIONAL: The deep reading problem
⚠️
AI skims. That's not an opinion — it's a published research finding.
In 2023, Stanford and UC Berkeley researchers published "Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts" (Liu et al., published in Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics, 2024). They found that LLMs retrieve information well from the beginning and end of long documents, but performance significantly degrades for content in the middle — even in models that technically support long context windows. The relationship follows a U-shaped curve. Upload a 60-page market research report and ask a general question — the AI will confidently answer based on the executive summary and conclusion, and quietly skip what's on page 34.
Source: Liu et al. (2024). Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts. Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics, 12, 157–173. arxiv.org/abs/2307.03172

These six techniques counteract the problem. Use them any time a document is more than 10–15 pages, or any time the answer matters enough that "probably right" isn't good enough.

1
Force a full structural scan before any answer
Ask AI to map the whole document before it answers any question — this forces a full pass, not just a skim of the intro and conclusion.
Before answering any questions about this document, read through the entire document and give me a section-by-section outline — the heading and one-sentence main point of each section. Do not skip appendices or footnotes. Start from the beginning and work through to the end.
2
Always demand the source quote
The single most reliable verification technique. If the AI found it in the document, it can quote it. If it can't quote it, it's confabulating.
For every claim you make about this document, quote the exact sentence or paragraph from the source material where you found it. If you cannot find a direct quote to support a claim, say so explicitly rather than paraphrasing from memory.
3
Use "find ALL instances" — not "does this mention X?"
"Does this document mention our competitor?" stops at the first hit. "Find EVERY place" forces comprehensive retrieval throughout the whole document.
Find and list EVERY place in this document where [competitor name / safety signal / dosing data / patient population] is mentioned — including in footnotes, appendices, and methodology sections. For each mention, quote the relevant sentence and note where it appears in the document.
4
Explicitly name the sections AI is most likely to skip
Research shows AI underperforms in the middle of long documents. Name those sections directly so AI cannot ignore them.
The most important information in this document is likely buried in the middle sections — not the executive summary or conclusion. Go back and specifically re-read the methodology, results, and discussion sections. Tell me what you find there about [specific topic] that wasn't mentioned in your initial summary.
5
Contradiction check — force a second pass with a different lens
After any answer, ask AI to re-read looking specifically for anything that qualifies or contradicts what it just said. Different lens = different retrieval.
Now re-read the document specifically looking for anything that contradicts, qualifies, or complicates what you just told me. Are there any caveats, limitations, or dissenting findings anywhere in the document — including footnotes and appendices — that would change how I should interpret this?
6
For very long documents — chunk it deliberately
Don't upload a 100-page document and ask one question. Process it section by section — higher accuracy per section than one sweeping pass over everything.
I'm going to work through this document with you section by section. Start by summarising only the Introduction and Background sections — what are the key claims, the stated objectives, and the framing assumptions? When you're done, I'll tell you which section to read next. Don't jump ahead.
